                                              • Perceptions of Musical Octaves Are Learned, Not Wired in the …

                                                • 4 comments
                                                • 12 days ago

                                                Robb —

                                                I’d be very cautious interpreting these results. The explanations for how we perceive many intervals on the musical scale are weak, and those perceptions vary across cultures, as do the scales themselves. But the octave is special. A normal instrument, including the human voice, resonates at the fundamental frequency and most or all of its harmonics. Those harmonics are the octaves above the fundamental. There is a very good reason why we regard octaves as equivalent: because when we play a note, we also play its octaves at the same time.

                                                If the Tsimané don’t usually sing in unison they might just lack the experience to transpose pitches accurately. This is absolutely a learned skill, and most westerners can transpose by an octave fairly easily but have much more difficulty transposing by any other interval. The Tsimané may also not regard the unison of octaves as desirable. It also makes a difference what instrument you use as the reference. Tuning forks strongly suppress the harmonics, and electronic signal generators can produce pure tones that lack harmonics altogether.
